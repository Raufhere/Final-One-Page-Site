Desert Crossroads The Afghan Cameleer's Legacy in Australia

A cameleer is a camel handler - someone who rides and, usually, cares for camels

Unloading camels, Port Augusta, 1890s. (Source: State Library of South Australia)

The first “Afghans” were brought to South Australia in 1838, to assist in the early exploration of the interior of the country. Camels played an essential role in navigating through the harsh desert terrain that horses would struggle with. The goldfields of Western Australia were thriving in the late 1890s.

Usually found in the desert, the gold mines were difficult for horses to ride because of the soft sand and extreme heat. Camel imports from other nations persisted in order to construct the necessary infrastructure, including roads and train lines. The men who transported and cared for these camels were referred to as "Afghan Cameleers."

Thousands of Afghani and Pakistani cameleers played an important role in shaping the Australian outback.

Since some of them weren't from Afghanistan, the name might be a little misleading. They also came from neighbouring countries such as Egypt, Iran, Turkey, India, and Pakistan. The majority of the cameleers were men and Muslims.

The cameleers actively participated in the community, yet despite this, they faced prejudice because of their looks and religious beliefs. Tensions within the communities developed as a result of the government's increased restrictions and negative portrayal of them in the newspapers.

Many cameleers were affected by the Immigration Restriction Act (White Australia Policy), which was in effect from 1901 to 1958. After visiting family overseas, several of them were forced to take a dictation exam, which led to their denial of entry into Australia, and many were denied naturalization.

Camel Caravans and the Spread of Islam: Australia's Untold Story

The cameleers helped to introduce Islam to Australia and built the first mosque in Marree, SA Hergott Springs (officially renamed Marree in 1883). The Afghan Cameleers were the founders Of Islam in Australia before the arrival of Cameleers no Muslim or apparent representation of Islam existed in Australia most immigrants were from Christian Europe and the Chinese immigration were mainly Confucian or Buddhist in the 1860s Afghans built the first Australian mosque originally a rough outback shelter.

Within 2 decades large mosques in Adelaide and Perth were built by the Afghan cameleers. although the Afghan cameleers were underpaid they were built with Cameleer donations for most of their time the Afghan cameleers were on the road and although far from their mosques 5 daily prayers were always maintained on lonely outback travels a small group of fellow Afghans would carry a copy of the Quran and under a shade of a tree set the scene for prayer their prayer was a crucial part of their life since it made them steadfast in their faith prayer fostered unity amongst them these Cameleers were the backbone of Australia's economy.

Camel Caravans: The Lifelines of Early Australian Transport Until the 1920s

The camels and their cameleers helped carry supplies inland for the mining and sheep industries, aided the building of the Overland Telegraph Line, the Canning Stock Route, major fence lines and the Trans-Australia and Central Australian railways. They carried pipe sections for the Goldfields Water Supply, supplied goods to inland towns, mining camps, sheep and cattle stations and also Aboriginal communities. Wagons hauled by Camels moved wool from sheep stations to railheads, pulled scoops in the construction of dams, and helped with ploughing and other farm work.

Timeline of Camels and Cameleers in Australia

  • 1840: The Phillips brothers, Henry Weston Phillips (1818–1898); George Phillips (1820– 1900); G M Phillips, bought nine camels at Tenerife in the Canary Islands
  • 1846: John Horrocks purchased “Harry” to explore the west of Lake Torrens in South Australia. The expedition was abandoned when “Harry” caused a fatal injury to Horrocks.
  • 1860: 9th June. First cameleers came to Melbourne with 24 camels purchased for the Burke and Wills expedition. Dost Mahomet was the senior cameleer, with Hassam Khan, Belooch4 Esau, a Hindu cameleer, resigned from the expedition when he realized he could not eat the meat (beef) provided for the expedition.
  • 1862: Samuel Stuckey, partner of Thomas Elder, traveled to Karachi to hire cameleers and purchase camels, but was unable to hire a ship to bring the camels to Australia. 1865 Samuel Stuckey returned from a second trip to Pakistan. He arrived on the Blackwell, on 31st December at Port Augusta with 31 cameleers and 124 camels. The first camel camp at Port Augusta was established.
  • 1870: Cameleer strike at Beltana Station
  • 1872: Completion of the Overland Telegraph between Darwin and Port Augusta.
  • 1873: William Gosse explored the Gibson Desert with Kamran, Jemma Khan, and Allanah as his cameleers. Gosse was the first European to sight Uluru and Kata Juta. 1875 Ernest Giles explored from Beltana to Geraldton and Perth with Saleh Mahomed and Halleem. Four expeditions from 1875 to 1876; the last one to Perth with 22 camels managed by Saleh Mahomed.
  • 1883: Charles Rasp thought he had discovered tin but in fact he had discovered silver, zinc, and lead. (Ion Idress) 1885 BHP founded.
  • 1885-6: David Lindsay traveled from Alice Springs to the Gulf of Carpentaria with Joorak Mahomed.
  • 1887: The first camels to arrive in Western Australia came on ships SS Abergeldie and SS Yeoman, both coming from Karachi, arriving in Fremantle in October 1887.
  • 1887 West Camel Camp Mosque built at Broken Hill. Arbitration Act cameleers declared as aliens and denied citizenship and union membership. 1890 Railway reached Oodnadatta.
  • 1888-91: Mosque built in Adelaide.
  • 1891: North Camel Camp Mosque built at Broken Hill, on a site which had been used for worship since at least 1887.
  • 1891-2: Lindsay explored Great Victoria and Gibson Deserts with Hadjih Shah Mahomet, Mahyedin, Alumgool, MahmoudAzim, Abdul and Joorak Mahomed handling the camels. Financed by Thomas Elder.
  • 1893: Discovery of gold at Kalgoorlie.
  • 1894: Horn Scientific Exploration Expedition to MacDonnell Ranges with Moosha and Guzzie Balooch managing the camels.
  • 1896-7: L.A. Wells led Calvert Expedition through Great Sandy and Gibson Deserts with cameleers Bejah Dervish and Said Ameer. Funded by Samuel Calvert.
  • 1898: Drought at White Cliffs forced residents to get supplies for a general store by camel from Broken Hill.
  • 1901: Federal Immigration Restriction Act. Survey for West Australian rabbit-proof fence. Hassan and Faizel Deen were cameleers for the survey team.
  • 1904: Sher Khan shot a romantic rival in Marree, returned to Farina where he was arrested, tried, and gaoled.
  • 1907: Afghan hawker, Chumpter Khan, shot five whites and killed a teamster.
  • 1912: East-West rail link started. Camels used to transport materials, supplies, and in the construction of the railway.

Camels and Culture: The Intersection of Cameleers and Aboriginal Life in Australia

Author of the book "Afghan Muslim Cameleers," historian and museum ethnographer Dr. Philip Jones, finds it fascinating how cameleers encountered Aboriginal culture and entered Australian history.

Dr. Jones claims that numerous young men traveled to Australia in the 19th century with the cameleers in an interview with SBS Urdu.

"Most of the young men who came to Australia to work as cameleers did so with their brothers. But many of them were neither attached to ladies from their own countries or married.

Many of these guys developed romantic relationships with Native American women.There are numerous instances of men developing more committed and lasting relationships with Aboriginal women, which resulted in births of children.

An Afghan and Aboriginal history

Goolam Badoola Rind of Balochistan, Pakistan, was one of the men who fled their homes and families.

Mr. Rind carried supplies while working in the goldfields of Western Australia.He first came across Marian Martin, a Wongi-Yamaji woman, there.At the period, cameleers were prohibited from bringing their families into the nation, thus Afghan men and Aboriginal women frequently got married. Neither party was permitted to use shared amenities like taverns or swimming pools after dark or to enter the town. Additionally, the Afghans were made to dwell in camps apart from the white population on the other side of the railroad tracks.

"Most of the young men who came to Australia to work as cameleers did so with their brothers. But many of them were neither attached to ladies from their own countries or married. On their travels, they frequently used Aboriginal trackers, and connections developed as a result of their cooperation and fair treatment. Goolam and Marian were wed in 1917 at the Perth Mosque, but because to the laws of the time, their union was not legally recognized. Because the marriage was viewed as an illegal act, it put them both in danger.

Gohar Rind, a Perth resident and the great-grandson of Goolam and Marian, asserted that acknowledging the importance of the cameleers' Labor may contribute to a more diverse Australia.

"If those efforts of the cameleers are understood, appreciated and acknowledged, then there would be better cohesion between different communities," added Rind.
